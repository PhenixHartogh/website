On **Monday 27 July 2026 at 2:00 PM AEST**, Australia runs its first national test of **AusAlert** — the new cell‑broadcast emergency warning system that goes fully live in **October 2026**. Here is a breakdown of the technology, why it is an upgrade, and specifically who might not get the alert.

---

### How It Works

**Cell‑broadcast technology, not SMS.**
- A signal is sent from every mobile tower to **all compatible devices** in a targeted geographic area — like a radio broadcast, not a text message to a specific number.
- The phone does **not** need an app, a SIM card, or the correct phone number saved. It just needs to be switched on and connected to a 4G/5G tower.
- The alert bypasses silent mode and Do Not Disturb: a **10‑second loud siren** plays automatically, followed by the on‑screen warning message.
- The system is **immune to network congestion** — during a disaster when everyone is trying to call or text, the broadcast channel stays open.

**The immediate benefit:** No more reliance on contact lists, no congestion delays, and wide geographic targeting.

---

### Concerns and Caveats

AusAlert has significant hardware, reception, and accessibility gaps. Here is what the test reveals.

#### 1. Flip Phones, “Safe Phones,” and Older Devices
- You need a **4G or 5G compatible device** with modern firmware that supports the GSM Cell Broadcast Service.
- Basic feature phones, **flip phones** (even 4G ones marketed as “senior” or “kids” phones), and many older Android handsets **do not** have the required chipset firmware to receive cell‑broadcast alerts.
- If your household relies on a “safe phone” for a child or elderly relative, **they will not receive the AusAlert siren or text** during a real emergency.

#### 2. Landlines and No Mobile Reception
- AusAlert only reaches devices connected to a mobile tower. **Landlines are excluded entirely.**
- Anyone in a **known mobile black spot** (common in regional, rural, and many parts of outer suburban Australia) will not receive the alert if there is no signal at that moment.
- The federal government still recommends a **battery‑powered AM/FM radio** (tuned to ABC Local Radio) as a backup warning source for these households.

#### 3. Language Barriers
- Alerts are composed by emergency services in **English**. There is no automatic translation or multilingual readout built into the cell‑broadcast standard.
- For culturally and linguistically diverse (CALD) communities, the siren signals urgency, but the specific instruction (“Evacuate now”, “Shelter in place”, “Do not enter the area”) may not be understood.
- Some states and territories have indicated they will explore pre‑translated templates, but this is not yet standardised across the national system.

#### 4. Missed Alerts (Timing & Off/On Behaviour)
- Cell‑broadcast is a **live‑only** system. It does not store the message on the network or your phone for later retrieval.
- If your phone is **switched off**, in **aeroplane mode**, or you are out of reception while the broadcast is active, **you will not get the alert**. Turning your phone back on hours later will not show a missed warning.
- This differs from the current SMS‑based Emergency Alert, which often sits in your inbox until you open it.

#### 5. State/Territory Coexistence
- States and territories are not required to switch exclusively to AusAlert. They can **run their own existing warning systems** (SMS, app‑based alerts, sirens, door‑knocking) alongside the federal system.
- This creates potential for overlapping, delayed, or inconsistent messaging depending on which system a resident is logged into.

---

### What the 27 July Test Actually Checks

- **End‑to‑end delivery** from the National Emergency Management Agency ([Nema](https://www.nema.gov.au/about-us/media-centre/prepare-ausalert-national-test-27-july-2026)) to the three major carriers (Telstra, Optus, TPG/Vodafone).
- Whether your specific **phone model and firmware** can process the cell‑broadcast channel (most recent iPhones and Android phones should work; older and basic devices will likely fail silently).
- The official line from NEMA: “If you do not receive the test alert, your device or carrier may not be compatible.” Check your phone’s settings or contact your manufacturer for cell‑broadcast support.

### Bottom Line

AusAlert fixes the network‑clog problem of SMS alerts and reaches a huge number of people instantly. But it is not a universal solution. **If your household uses a landline, a basic phone, or lives in a fringe‑reception area, AusAlert alone is not a reliable warning source.** Have a backup: a digital radio, the local emergency service app with push notifications, and a spoken plan with neighbours or family.
