Prime Minister Anthony Albanese has formally responded to the landmark Islamophobia report delivered earlier this year by the Australian Human Rights Commission. The report, commissioned in the wake of rising anti-Muslim sentiment across the country, made 19 recommendations spanning education, media standards, workplace protections, and community safety.

**Key Commitments from the Government**

The Prime Minister accepted the majority of the report's findings in principle, with several immediate actions announced:

- **A dedicated Islamophobia unit** will be established within the Attorney-General's Department, tasked with monitoring incidents and coordinating national responses.
- **$15 million in funding** over four years has been allocated for community resilience grants, education programs, and support services for victims of anti-Muslim discrimination.
- **A review of hate speech laws** will be undertaken, with a focus on closing gaps that currently allow religious vilification to go unchecked in several jurisdictions.
- The government has committed to **national consistency in anti-vilification laws**, urging state and territory leaders to harmonise protections for religious minorities.

**What the Report Found**

The AHRC report documented a sharp increase in Islamophobic incidents since October 2023, including physical assaults, online harassment, and vandalism of mosques and Islamic schools. It noted that Muslim women — particularly those wearing visible religious dress — are disproportionately targeted. The report also identified systemic underreporting, with many victims citing distrust of authorities or fear of reprisal.

**Reception and Criticism**

Community leaders have broadly welcomed the response, though some have called for stronger legislative action. The Islamic Council of Victoria described the announcement as "a meaningful first step" but stressed that without enforceable hate speech laws at the federal level, the impact will remain limited.

On the other side, the opposition has questioned the need for a faith-specific body, arguing that existing anti-discrimination frameworks should be sufficient. The government has pushed back, pointing to the report's evidence that general protections have failed to prevent a sustained rise in attacks.

**Next Steps**

The Islamophobia unit is expected to begin operations within the next quarter. A cross-departmental taskforce will oversee implementation of the remaining recommendations, with a progress report due before the end of the year.
